# Write a Persuasive Business Case

**Category:** Writing & Communication
**Tags:** persuasion, business-case, proposal, decision, exec-communication

## What it does

Turns an idea, ask, or investment into a tight, persuasive business case — framed around the problem, the value, and the decision you want, so stakeholders can say yes quickly.

## Prompt

```text
You are a sharp business strategist and writer. Help me make the case below
compelling and decision-ready.

Produce a structured business case with:
1. **The ask** — one clear sentence stating exactly what I want approved.
2. **The problem / opportunity** — why this matters now, with the cost of inaction.
3. **The proposal** — what we'd do, in plain language.
4. **The value** — expected benefits, quantified where possible (time, money,
   risk, growth); separate "hard" from "soft" benefits.
5. **What it takes** — resources, cost, timeline, and who's involved.
6. **Risks & mitigations** — the top objections a skeptic would raise, answered.
7. **The recommendation** — a confident closing line and clear next step.

Rules:
- Lead with the ask and the value, not the background.
- Be specific; replace vague claims ("improves efficiency") with concrete outcomes.
- Keep it concise enough to fit on one page or one short email.

What I'm proposing: {{your idea or ask}}
The audience / decision-maker: {{who needs to approve it and what they care about}}
Key facts, numbers, or context: {{costs, benefits, deadlines, constraints}}
```

## Example

**Input:** "I want budget to pilot a new onboarding program," aimed at a VP who cares about ramp time and retention.

**Output:** A one-page case opening with the budget ask and expected ramp-time gain, a crisp problem statement, quantified benefits, cost and timeline, the top three objections pre-answered, and a clear recommendation.

## Tips

- Give it the decision-maker's priorities — the case will speak directly to what they value.
- Ask it for both a one-page version and a three-sentence verbal pitch.
